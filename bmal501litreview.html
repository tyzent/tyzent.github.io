Literature Review: Effective Teams in the Workplace

Introduction

There has been a significant transformation in the last four decades from work organized around individual jobs to team-based work structures (Andrejczuk et al., 2018). Modern organizational teams face significant challenges succeeding in highly competitive climates with ever-changing challenges. Modern day organizations are more diverse, dynamic, and geographically disconnected than at any point in history. Team leaders must always be striving to improve overall culture, composition, and productivity of their teams while maintaining awareness of individual employee wellbeing. Building effective teams in the workplace is critical to organizational success. Outcomes can be affected by easily measurable one-time decisions, while others require a less tangible and delicate balance. Individual team members can be demotivated by problems with colleagues, a perceived lack of leadership integrity, and ineffective organizational structures. The primary purpose of a leader is to remove roadblocks for effective team success. The leadership style, personality traits, and structures of leaders and leadership teams are critical to creating effective workplace teams. Integral to efficient structure is the type of team and both how they work within the business, and how they work with each other. Positive working environments and workplace resilience are correlated and necessary to create highly effective teams.

Teamwork

Teamwork is the process of working collectively in a group to achieve a desired goal. One element of teamwork is how a team fits within the larger organization, and another is how that team functions and works together. Teamwork brings people together and motivates them to rely on each other to achieve a goal. Effective teamwork makes progress easier, and allows the group to overcome obstacles that might have stymied an individual. A critical element of teamwork is communication, both within the team itself, and how the team communicates with the larger organization.

Team structure refers to the composition of an individual team and how they connect to outside elements. The structure defines how a company’s activities will meet its goals. Effective teams are important to organizational competitiveness, with especially critical elements such as team composition, team formation, and their relationship with overall performance. It is crucial to have heterogeneous team members to maximize team performance heterogeneous team (Andrejczuk et al., 2018). A heterogeneous team is composed of diverse individuals in terms of their abilities and experiences.

The primary way for teams to work together is with consistent meetings. At their best these get-togethers can be a good way for all members of a team to be on the same page and make sure they are working collaboratively, and at their worst they can be frustrating and unhelpful. Workplace meetings are a topic of significant popular debate, especially with the need for flexibility in light of the COVID-19 pandemic. Effective communication and collaboration are critical to team effectiveness, and can have significant positive and negative impacts on an organization. There is a need to further explore the topic, with a focus on meetings as rituals wherein groups and organizations form cultures, identities, and climates (Mroz et al., 2018).

At its core a team is a shared commitment, and strives for something greater than the sum of individual abilities. When teams are working together at their critical effectiveness, they are able to enter a team flow state. Team flow is a state of mind in which a group becomes fully immersed in a collective activity. Work produced during a flow state tends to be of higher quality and more creative than work produced outside of these periods of deep work. Benefits of high engagement include a strong connection to the team and organization, and a better team performance. A team is more likely to enter a flow state when everyone sees a challenge or opportunity for action that meets the top of their collective skill level, and therefore promotes deep engagement (van den Hout et al., 2018).

Team Composition

Team composition refers to the overall mix of characteristics of people in a team, with an emphasis on the leader. Key factors include leadership styles and personality traits. A well-composed team has individuals with complementary knowledge and skills as well as leadership style and personality traits that are not only compatible, but elevate the overall potential for success. Deep-level composition variables like personality traits and leadership styles tend to have a stronger and longer lasting influence on teamwork and performance than surface-level variables like demographics.

Leadership is a major factor in the growth, effectiveness, and success of organizations. Effective leaders are able to adapt their leadership styles depending on their understanding of the organizational situation. House’s path-goal theory suggests that the effectiveness of leadership behavior is contingent on organizational context. The leader’s ability to clarify goals and motivate employees depend on how well they reduce role ambiguity and provide adequate rewards, and a leader’s ability to motivate their team involves clarifying the path, reducing roadblocks and pitfalls, and facilitating personal satisfaction. Leaders are effective when they are able to clarify linkages between effort and goal attainment, and provide necessary resources and support for achieving work goals. Successful teams develop the leadership skills of each member so that they can proactively address change. Under a shared leadership model, all members should be held accountable for team performance and be encouraged to communicate concerns to the team (Satterlee, 2019). House stressed the importance of shared leadership, and his theory indicates that leaders can adapt their behavior to meet the specific needs of the organization. Shared transformational leadership has a critical role in the safety behaviors of team members (Lyubykh et al., 2022). Team members working under a shared transformational leadership model are more likely to comply with safety regulations.

The key factors of an effective workplace teams are skill, experience and personality. While business often consider skills and expertise, taking personality traits into account can be a determining factor in the team’s success. The five major personality traits are openness, conscientiousness, extraversion, agreeableness, and neuroticism. Some industry leaders suggest that complimentary traits within a team are helpful, but that too much diversity in personality can be a negative factor in team effectiveness, as it can lead to a lack of understanding and empathy. There is significant scholarly research regarding extroversion as a personality trait among leaders, and limited assessments of positive elements of introverted leaders. Extraverted leaders may be poor team contributors (Blevins et al., 2021). More research should be conducted on introversion, to better understand how introverts can continue to add value within their workplace teams. Introversion and extroversion are important traits to consider when discussing effective teams in the workplace, and the positive characteristics of each in a team environment.

Team Culture

Team culture is influenced by and built through the work environment, and impacts the ability of a team to band together for a common purpose and remain flexible when facing adversity. Team culture is a combination of values, beliefs, and behaviors that members share and the outcomes resulting from the factors. A strong team culture promotes loyalty and engagement in members and attracts talented outside individuals.

One goal of an effective workplace team is to create a positive working environment with a person-centered culture. Modern competitive organizations are shifting away from negative outcomes and focusing on creating conditions that promote a positive environment characteristic of flourishing human condition (Geue, 2018). These companies endeavor to develop a climate that promotes employee well-being.

Person-centered workplace cultures and effective teamwork have been linked to organizations achieving optimal outcomes (Masimula et al., 2021). A person-centered workplace is a respectful, safe, and engaging environment for employees. This type of culture would be a shift for some businesses that are production or profit focused. Employers should put their employees first, not customers, profit, or production.

Workplace team resilience is the ability of a team to respond to change and disruption in a flexible and innovative manner. Employees often face high pressure in their work environments, and adverse events are inevitable when working with people. Workplace resilience is a potential key factor in maintaining effective performance on both a team and individual level. A group composed of resilient individuals may not necessarily form a resilient team (Hartwig et al., 2020). There is a relationship between the two but there are other determining factors like culture, composition, and leadership. Resilient teams are able to band together to achieve organizational success, but the system and culture are as important as the team composition (Hibbert et al., 2018).

Successful Outcomes

The goal of any team is to achieve a set goal, and there are various measures to qualify success. Diverse perspectives and thought processes can be critical to team innovation. Difference-driven inquiry is the process of deliberation that aims to voice difference and emplace healthy conflict, as opposed to ideational tools that might not be effective without high team cohesion. A team’s ability to innovate has less to do with the composition of a team that the structures of the team’s operations (Moreau, 2020). Regular review of a group’s performance through team meetings and one-on-one performance appraisals will ensure that progress is being made. After the outcomes are adequately understood thoroughly, it is important for leaders to regularly recognize, reward, and celebrate team and individual performance.

Conclusion

It is critical for a leader to understand, evaluate, and measure the effectiveness of workplace teams. Key factors include the composition, structures, and measurable outcomes. Each member of the team can bring a unique perspective and a complimentary set of personality traits and leadership styles. Communication is critical to overall team effectiveness, both within the team and with the larger organizational structure. An effective workplace team is built of goal-focused individuals with specialized expertise and complementary skills who collaborate, innovate, and produce consistently superior results. They relentlessly pursue excellent performance through shared goals, shared leadership, collaboration, open communication, and clear expectations. A leader looking to build an effective workplace team should focus on building trust, working with integrity, and encourage healthy organizational conflict. Effective teamwork requires openness, trust, and respect among colleagues.
